From Homophobia to Founding OutBound

By Eric Arellano

I grew up disliking gay people, so I was in for quite the journey towards accepting myself as gay. My name's Eric, and I'm a junior in high school in Tucson, Arizona. I started OutBound because of the experiences I went through, and have since been excited to help people figure out their identity and come out of the closet. Outside of OutBound and school, I'm a big time fan of video games, alternative-ish music, and the Colbert Report.

I grew up in a conservative, religious family in the Bay Area. I was out with my mom in 2008 trying to stop California's marriage equality by going door-to-door for Yes on Prop 8. Now outside of frustrating unacceptance towards the LGBTQ community, my family was pretty great and loving. But still, this stance made it difficult for me to accept my sexual orientation sophomore year of high school.

After ignoring my sexuality during middle school and freshman year, I ended up finally dealing with it in 2012 as more of my friends began to date. I at first was embarrassed and scared, but I eventually realized my orientation was just one factor of who I am.

I wasn't comfortable telling others, but I soon told one of my closest friends to try to find support. With a great response from her, I moved on to telling my parents, knowing that the response wouldn't be pretty. My parents were mostly concerned that I was confused and in a stage, and in response encouraged me to "talk to experts." I was scared this would put me back in the closet, after finally finding some self-acceptance, so I naturally resisted anything but acceptance of how I identify. My parents meant to protect me from what they saw as a difficult life from persecution for being gay, but in the process I felt rejected. My older brothers, too, were very uncomfortable with my orientation.

Fortunately, the cliche "it gets better" really is true. While my family was gradually becoming more comfortable, I began to tell more of my friends and peers. Even my group of guy friends who occasionally made gay jokes was very empathetic and accepting. They acted like nothing really changed, what I was hoping for. By junior year of high school, my family had nearly reached acceptance. Things still aren't entirely perfect, but we once again trust and love each other. After moving from the Bay Area to Tucson, Arizona this past summer, I've been happy to find support among my peers for living honestly.

There were some ups and downs, but I am incredibly grateful that I now not only found acceptance for gay people but that identify as gay.
