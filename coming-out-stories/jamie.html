Coming Out:
A Lifelong Journey

By Jamie Kerwin

My name is Jamie Kerwin and I live in Juno, Alaska. I am a full-time AmeriCorps volunteer serving at the LGBTQ non-profit organization, Identity. I am Identity’s youth engagement specialist, and I work with all of our youth programs. I am very passionate about improving the social and emotional lives of LGBTQ youth and young adults. I want LGBTQ youth to be able to have experiences that were different from my own.

I grew up in an extremely strict and religious household. My family belonged to the Church of Christ, which is a very unaccepting Christian denomination. We went to church, rain or shine, three times a week. Honestly, I didn’t even know what being gay meant until I was 12. I started to comprehend it more as I aged and approached high school, but I never heard positive messages about being gay. As the gay rights movement began to expand and marriage equality became a more relevant political issue, I would frequently hear negative messages concerning marriage equality in the Sunday sermon. When I was 16, I asked my mom what she would do if I came out as gay. She told me she would kick me out of the house. Of course, I don’t think she would have actually done this. However, I do believe that she would have sent me to reparative therapy. I didn’t realize my feelings towards women were real until college. I didn’t have a framework for understanding these feelings until I started to become a more open-minded individual.

Accepting myself was a hard process. I had a lot of internalized homophobic attitudes that I am ashamed of today. I always stood up for gay rights, but I never realized how homophobic I actually was until I began questioning my sexuality at the age of 19. I slowly came out as bisexual/pansexual to a lot of my inner circles. Most people took the message with shock or seemed uncomfortable by it. Even my best friend, who was gay, told me that he didn’t believe in bisexuality. Instead of supporting me in my confusion, he made me feel worse. Needless to say, it took a while before I could sort out the people who really supported me. My sister was the only member of my direct family who has always been supportive throughout this whole process. Coming out to my mom was very difficult. She is still not supportive of my sexuality, so we have a very limited relationship. It was hard to accept myself when I was constantly being told my soul was in jeopardy, so I have isolated myself from her. I was never able to come out to my dad, who died this past November. It is a regret of mine.

Although my coming out process was very difficult, things are much better for me now. I currently identify as a lesbian in my work circles and queer in my youth circles. I am also a feminist. Personally, I think the process of unacceptance has been a positive factor in my life. It has exposed the people in my life who truly matter and support me as an individual. A lot of people in my life who I did not expect to accept me have stepped forward and extended their support. Accepting yourself and coming out is a lifelong process. I’m proud of myself for having the courage to take control of my life. I love being gay!
